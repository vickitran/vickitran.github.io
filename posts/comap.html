Tackling Big Data in 72 Hours

Unsurprisingly, I signed up as well convinced a few of my friends to participate in the annual Consortium for Mathematics and Its Applications Mathematical Contest in Modelling. Typically, the committee poses a selection of problems to which undergraduate students develop, test and write a mathematical solution.

There was a special addition to this year's line-up, one of the problems incorporated Big Data. The challenge was to devise a donation strategy for a post-secondary education charity. The most interesting aspect was designing an appropriate return on investment. The available data sets were U.S. National Center on Education Statistics and College Scorecard data set.

Cleaning the Data:

While the committee supposedly provided snapshots of data, our team quickly realized the data was too incomplete for the problem at hand. We set out to collect the required variables for nearly 7000 schools.

A teammate collected all the relevant data, filtered out smaller schools, specific designations, and level of institution. The goal was to have a uniform set of school to avoid skewing the ranking system.

Ranking the Data:

We created a benchmark of the top 10 schools, who had lowered the poverty rate for students coming in. Post-secondary education is often seen as the class-equalizer providing opportunities for individuals to guarantee higher annual income. Therefore we picked the schools that performed the best in

  • Poverty Rate, Census Data
  • Share of students making over $25,000/yr 10 years after entry.

A simple relative change formula was used and the schools with the highest score placed top of the list. Then the other schools on the list were compared to the benchmarks and classified into ten different clusters. The variables values were also normalized.
